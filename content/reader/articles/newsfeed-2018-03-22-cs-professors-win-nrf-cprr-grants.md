<div class="newsitem"><h1>CS Professors win NRF CPRR grants</h1><div class="newsdate"><span class="day"> Thursday </span><span class="date"> 22 </span><span class="month">March</span><span class="year">2018</span></div><p class="noimage"><img src="/cs-assets/newsfeed/nrflogo.jpg" alt="NRF"></p><p>Professors L. van Zijl, W. Visser, and B. Fischer have recently been awarded competitive research (CPRR) grants from the National Research Foundation. These include bursaries for MSc and PhD students.</p><p>Prof. <a href="http://www.cs.sun.ac.za/~lvzijl/">Lynette van Zijl</a> was awarded a grant for her research project ‘‘Hybrid extended symmetric difference nondeterministic finite automata’’. This research concerns formal models of computation, and finding more space-efficient representations for known models. In particular, the cyclic behaviour of computations is analysed, with emphasis on the cases where symmetry plays a role in behaviour. In the extension of known models, the effect of backtracking in the input in various ways, will be considered. Such analysis is important from both a theoretical and a practical point of view, as it may be applicable to topics such as random number generation, perfect hashing, and others.</p><p>Prof. <a href="http://www.cs.sun.ac.za/~wvisser/">Willem Visser’s</a> project ‘‘Applications of Model Counting to Software Testing’’ proposes a novel approach to improve the testing of software, which increasingly runs our lives—and is sometimes closer to ruining them than we might be aware of… We are really at the mercy of the software testing process to keep us from potential disaster. This work will use a new approach to quantifying exactly how likely a program is to exhibit a specific behaviour, called probabilistic symbolic execution, to calculate the number of inputs that will follow specific paths through the code. This allows us to quantify how well a program is actually tested.</p><p>Prof. <a href="http://www.cs.sun.ac.za/~bfischer/">Bernd Fischer</a> received funding for his project ‘‘Grammar-based generation of passing and failing test cases for complex domains’’. This will investigate new methods to generate test suites from formal grammars describing the structure of input domains. This will simplify the construction of test suites (ie test inputs and expected outputs) for applications where the program inputs are complex and must satisfy many structural (eg XML schemas) and non-structural (eg data encoding) constraints.</p></div>
